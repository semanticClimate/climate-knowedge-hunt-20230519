Agreement and the SDGs provided a new international context, but global intergovernmental 11
cooperation has been under intense stress. Growing direct impacts of climate change are unambiguous 12
and movements of protest and activism – in countries and transnational organisations at many levels – 13
have grown. Global emissions growth had slowed but not stopped up to 2018/19, albeit with more 14
diverse national trends. Growing numbers of countries have adopted ‘net zero’ CO

Developed countries are amongst the most unsustainable in terms of overall consumption, but also face 23
social constraints particularly arising from distributional impacts of climate policies. 24
The assessment of the key drivers for, and barriers against mitigation undertaken in this chapter 25
underscore the complexity and multidimensional nature of climate mitigation. Historically, much of the 26
academic analysis of mitigating climate change, particularly global approaches, has focused on 27
modelling costs and pathways, and discussion about ‘optimal’ policy instruments. Developments since 28
AR5 have continued to highlight the role of a wide range of factors intersecting the political, economic, 29
social and institutional domains. Yet despite such complexities, there are signs of progress emerging 30
from years of policy effort in terms of technology, social attitudes, emission reductions in some 31
countries, with tentative signs of impact on the trajectory of global emissions. The challenge remains 32
how best to urgently scale up and speed-up the climate mitigation effort at all scales –from local to 33
global – to achieve the level of mitigation needed to address the problem as indicated by climate science. 34
A related challenge is how to ensure that mitigation effort and any associated benefits of action are 35
distributed fairly within and between countries and aligned to the overarching objective of global 36
sustainable development. Lastly, globally effective and efficient mitigation will require international 37
co-operation especially in the realms of finance, and technology. 38
Multiple frameworks of analytic assessment, adapted to the realities of climate change mitigation, are 39
therefore required. We identified four main groups. Aggregate economic frameworks – including 40
environmental costs or goals, and with due attention to implied behavioural, distributional and dynamic 41
assumptions - can provide insights about trade-offs, cost-effectiveness and policies for delivering 42
agreed goals. Ethical frameworks are equally essential to inform both international and domestic 43
