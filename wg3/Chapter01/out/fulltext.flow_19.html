Post-pandemic recovery pathways provide an opportunity to attract finance into accelerated and 14
transformative low-carbon public investment (15.2, 15.6.3). In most countries, COVID-19 has increased 15
unemployment and/or state-supported employment. There is a profound difference between short-term 16
‘bail-outs’ to stem unemployment, and the orientation of new public investment. The public debt is mirrored 17
by large pools of private capital. During deep crises like that of the COVID-19, economic multipliers of 18
stimulus packages can be high (Hepburn et al., 2020), so much so that fiscal injections can then generate 19
multipliers from 1.5 to 2.5, weakening the alleged crowding-out effect of public stimulus (Auerbach and 20
Gorodnichenko 2012; Blanchard and Leigh 2013; Section 15.2.3 in Chapter 15). 21
Recovery packages are motivated by assessments that investing in can boost the macroeconomic 22
effectiveness (‘multipliers’) of public spending, crowd-in and revive private investment (Hepburn et al. 23
2020). There are clear reasons why a low-carbon response can create more enduring jobs, better aligned to 24
future growth sectors: by also crowding-in and reviving private investment (e.g. from capital markets and 25
institutional investors, including the growing profile of Environment and Social Governance (ESG) and 26
green bond markets (15.6)), this can boost the effectiveness of public spending (IMF 2020). Stern and 27
Valero (Stern and Valero 2021) argue that investment in low-carbon innovation and its diffusion, 28
complemented by investments in sustainable infrastructure, are key to shape environmentally sustainable 29
and inclusive growth in the aftermath of the COVID-19 pandemic crisis. This would be the case both for 30
high-income economies on the global innovation frontier, and to promote sustainable development in 31
poorer economies. 32
