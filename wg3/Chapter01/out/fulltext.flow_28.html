1.4.5
Political economy
1
The politics of interest (most especially economic interest) of key actors at subnational, national and global 2
level can be important determinants of climate (in)action (O’Hara 2009; Lo 2010; Tanner and Allouche 3
2011; Sovacool et al. 2015; Clapp et al. 2018; Lohmann 2017; Newell and Taylor 2018; Lohmann 2019). 4
Political economy approaches can be crudely divided into “economic approaches to politics”, and those 5
used by other social scientists (Paterson and P‐Laberge 2018). The former shows how electoral concerns 6
lead to weak treaties (Battaglini and Harstad 2016) and when policy negotiations cause status-quo biases 7
and the use of inefficient policy instruments (Austen-Smith et al. 2019) or delays and excessive 8
harmonization (Harstad 2007). The latter emphasises the central role of structures of power, production, 9
and a commitment to economic growth and capital accumulation in relation to climate action, given the 10
historically central role of fossil fuels to economic development and the deep embedding of fossil energy 11
in daily life (Malm 2015; Huber 2012; Di Muzio 2015; Newell and Paterson 2010). 12
The economic centrality of fossil fuels raises obvious questions regarding the possibility of decarbonisation. 13
Economically, this is well understood as a problem of decoupling. But the constraint is also political, in 14
terms of the power of incumbent fossil fuel interests to block initiatives towards decarbonisation (Newell 15
and Paterson 2010; Geels 2014; Jones and Levy 2009). The effects of climate policy are key considerations 16
in deciding the level of policy ambition and direction and strategies of states (Alam et al. 2013; Ibikunle 17
and Okereke 2014; Lo 2010), regions (Goldthau and Sitter 2015); and business actors (Wittneben et al. 18
2012) and there is a widespread cultural assumption that continued fossil fuel use is central to this (Strambo 19
and Espinosa 2020). Decarbonisation strategies are often centred around projects to develop new sources 20
of economic activity: carbon markets creating new commodities to trade profit from (Newell and Paterson 21
2010); the investment generated in new urban infrastructure (Whitehead 2013); innovations in a range of 22
new energy technologies (Fankhauser et al. 2013; Lachapelle et al. 2017; Meckling and Nahm 2018). 23
One factor limiting the ambition of climate policy has been the ability of incumbent industries to shape 24
government action on climate change (Newell and Paterson 1998; Breetz et al. 2018; Jones and Levy 2009; 25
Geels 2014). Incumbent industries are often more concentrated than those benefiting from climate policy 26
and lobby more effectively to prevent losses than those who would gain (Meng and Rode 2019). Drawing 27
upon wider networks (Brulle 2014), campaigns by oil and coal companies against climate action in the US 28
and Australia are perhaps the most well-known and largely successful of these (Brulle et al. 2020; Stokes 29
2020; Mildenberger 2020; Pearse 2017) although similar dynamics have been demonstrated for example in 30
Brazil and South Africa (Hochstetler 2020), Canada (Harrison 2018), Norway , or Germany (Fitzgerald et 31
al. 2019). In other contexts, resistance by incumbent companies is more subtle but nevertheless has 32
weakened policy design on emissions trading systems (Rosembloom and Markard 2020), and limited the 33
development of alternative fuelled automobiles (Wells and Nieuwenhuis 2012; Levy and Egan 2003). 34
The interaction of politics, power and economics is central in explaining why countries with higher per-35
capita emissions, which logically have more opportunities to reduce emissions, in practice often take the 36
opposite stance, and conversely, why some low-emitting countries may find it easier to pursue climate 37
action because they have fewer vested interests in high-carbon economies. These dynamics can arise from 38
the vested interest of State-owned Enterprises (Polman 2015; Wright and Nyberg 2017; Wittneben et al. 39
2012), the alignment and coalitions of countries in climate negotiations (Gupta 2016; Okereke and Coventry 40
2016), and the patterns of opposition to or support for climate policy among citizens (Swilling et al. 2016; 41
Ransan-Cooper et al. 2018; Turhan et al. 2019; Baker 2015; Heffron and McCauley 2018). 42
