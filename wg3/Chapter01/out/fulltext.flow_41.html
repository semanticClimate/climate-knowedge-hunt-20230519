The key insight is that pursuing climate goals in the context of sustainable development requires holistic 19
thinking including on how to measure well-being, serious consideration of the notion of ecological 20
limits, at least some level of decoupling and certainly choices and decision-making approaches that 21
exploit and maximise the synergy and minimises the trade-off between climate mitigation and other 22
sustainable development goals. It also requires consideration of equity and justice within and between 23
countries. However, ideas of a synergistic relationship between development and climate mitigation 24
can sometimes offer limited practical guidelines for reconciling the tensions that are often present in 25
practical policy making (Dale et al. 2015b; Ferguson et al. 2014; Kasztelan 2017; Kotzé 2018). 26
1.6.3
Climate Mitigation, Equity and the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)
27
Climate action can be conceptualised as both a stand-alone and cross-cutting issue in the 2030 SDGs 28
(Makomere and Liti Mbeva 2018), given that several of the other goals such as ending poverty (Goal 29
1), zero hunger (Goal 2), good health and wellbeing (Goal 3), affordable and clean energy (Goal 7) 30
among many others are related to climate change (see Figure 3.39 in Chapter 3). 31
In addition to galvanising global collective action, the SDGs provide concrete themes, targets and 32
indicators for measuring human progress to sustainability (Kanie and Biermann 2017). The SDGs also 33
provide a basis for exploring the synergies and trade-offs between sustainable development and climate 34
change mitigation (Makomere and Liti Mbeva 2018; Mainali et al. 2018; Fuso Nerini et al. 2018; 35
Pradhan et al. 2017). Progress to date (Sachs et al. 2016) shows fulfilling SDGs is a challenge for all 36
groups of countries – developed and developing – even though the challenge differs between countries 37
and regions (Pradhan et al. 2017). 38
