1.7.1.1
Cost-benefit and cost-effectiveness analysis 3
Such global aggregate economic studies coalesce around two main questions. One, as pioneered by 4
Nordhaus (1992; 2008) attempts to monetize overall climate damages and mitigation costs so as to 5
strike a ‘cost-benefit optimum’ pathway. More detailed and empirically-grounded ‘cost-effectiveness 6
analysis’ explores pathways that would minimise mitigation costs (IPCC 2014a section 2.5; Ekholm 7
2014; Weyant 2017) for given targets (e.g. as agreed in international negotiations, see Section 3.2 in 8
Chapter 3). Both approaches recognise that resources are limited, and climate change competes with 9
other priorities in government policymaking, and are generally examined with some form of Integrated 10
Assessment Model (IAMs: see 1.5, and Appendix C). Depending on the regional disaggregation of the 11
modelling tools used and on the scope of the analyses, these studies may or may not address 12
distributional aspects within and across nations associated with climate policies (Bauer et al. 2020). 13
For at least 10-15 years after the first computed global cost-benefit estimate (Nordhaus 1992), the 14
dominant conclusions from these different approaches seemed to yield very different recommendations, 15
with cost-benefit studies suggesting lenient mitigation compared to the climate targets typically 16
recommended from scientific risk assessments (Weyant 2017). Over the past 10-15 years, literature has 17
made important strides towards reconciling these two approaches, both in the analytic methods and the 18
conclusions arising. 19
Damages and risks Incorporating impacts which may be extremely severe but are uncertain (known as 20
“fat tails” (Weitzman 2009, 2011), strengthens the economic case for ambitious action to avoid risks of 21
extreme climate impacts (Ackerman et al. 2010; Dietz and Stern 2015; Fankhauser et al. 2013). The 22
salience of risks has also been amplified by improved understanding of climate ‘tipping points’ 23
(Lontzek et al. 2015; Lenton et al. 2019); valuations should reflect that cutting emissions reduces not 24
only average expected damages, but also the risk of catastrophic events (IWG 2021). 25

Distribution of impacts. The economic damages from climate change at the nationally aggregated and 37
subnational level are very diverse (Moore et al. 2017; Ricke et al. 2018; Carleton et al. 2020). A 'global 38
damage function’ necessarily implies aggregating impacts across people and countries with different 39
levels of income, and over generations, a process which obscures the strategic considerations that drive 40
climate policy making (Keohane and Oppenheimer 2016). Economics acknowledges there is no single, 41
objectively-defined such ‘social welfare function’ (IPCC 1995, 2014a). This applies also to distribution 42
of responses; both underline the relevance of equity (next section) and global negotiations to determine 43
national and collective objectives. 44
