1.5
Emissions Scenarios and Illustrative Mitigation Pathways (IMPs)
10
Scenarios are a powerful tool for exploring an uncertain future world against the background of alternative 11
choices and development. Scenarios can be constructed using both narrative and quantitative methods. 12
When these two methods are combined they provide complementary information and insights. Quantitative 13
and narrative models are frequently used to represent scenarios to explore choices and challenges. The 14
IPCC has a long history of assessing scenarios (Nakicenovic et al. 2000; van Vuuren et al. 2011, 2014; see 15
also section 1.6 of AR6 WGI for a history of scenarios within the IPCC). This WGIII assessment employs 16
a wide range of qualitative and quantitative scenarios including quantitative scenarios developed through a 17
wide and heterogeneous set of tools ranging from spreadsheets to complex computational models (Annex 18
III provides further discussion and examples of computational models). 19
The concept of an illustrative pathway (IP) was introduced in IPCC Special Report on 1.5 (IPCC 2018b) 20
to highlight a subset of the quantitative scenarios, drawn from a larger pool of published literature, with 21
specific characteristics that would help represent some of the key findings emerging from the assessment 22
in terms of different strategies, ambitions and options available to achieve the Paris goals. 23
Integrated Assessment Models (IAMs) are the primary tools for quantitatively evaluating the technological 24
and macro-economic implications of decarbonisation, particularly for global long-term pathways. They 25
broadly divide into ‘stylized aggregate benefit-cost models’, and more complex, ‘detailed process’ IAMs 26
(Weyant 2017), often mirroring the benefit-cost and cost-effective approaches outlined in 1.7.1, with more 27
detailed classification in eg. Nikas et al. (2019). IAMs embody a number of structural and socio-28
demographic assumptions and include multiple modelling approaches, ranging from economic optimising 29
behaviour to simulation (See Annex III). Detailed process models can include energy system models used 30
to analyse decarbonisation and ‘net zero’ scenarios by international agencies (eg. IEA 2020a). 31
Calculating cost-effective trajectories towards given goals typically involves detailed process IAMs. Often 32
these calculate the dynamic portfolio of technologies consistent with a given climate target. Some track 33
records of technology forecasting in IAMs are outlined in Chapters 2.5.4, and Box 16.1. Climate targets 34
may be imposed in models in a variety of ways that include, but are not limited to, constraints on emissions 35
or cumulated emissions (carbon budgets), and the pricing of emissions. The time-path of mitigation costs 36
calculated through these models may be translated into ‘shadow prices’ that (like the social-cost-of-carbon) 37
offer a benchmark to assess the cost-effectiveness of investments, as used by some governments and 38
companies (1.8.2). 39
