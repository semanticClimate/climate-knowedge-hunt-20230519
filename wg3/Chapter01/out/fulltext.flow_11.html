The Paris Agreement (PA). The Paris Agreement aims to “hold the increase in the global average 3
temperature to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and pursuing efforts to limit the temperature 4
increase to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels” (UNFCCC 2015), alongside goals for adaptation (IPCC 5
WGII), and ‘aligning financial flows’ (below) , so as “to strengthen the global response to the threat of 6
climate change, in the context of sustainable development and efforts to eradicate poverty.” 7
The Paris Agreement is predicated on encouraging progressively ambitious climate action from all 8
countries on the basis of Nationally Determined Contributions (Rajamani 2016; Clémençon 2016). The 9
NDC approach requires countries to set their own level of ambitions for climate change mitigation but 10
within a collaborative and legally binding process to foster ambition towards the agreed goals (Falkner 11
2016a; Bodansky 2016). The PA entered into force in November 2016 and as of February 2021 it has 12
190 Parties (out of 197 Parties to the UNFCCC). 13
The PA also underlines “the principle of common but differentiated responsibilities and respective 14
capabilities, in the light of different national circumstances” (PA Article 2 para 2), and correspondingly 15
that “developed country Parties should continue taking the lead by undertaking economy-wide absolute 16
emission reductions”. It states that developing country Parties should continue enhancing their 17
mitigation efforts, and are encouraged to move over time towards economy-wide emission reduction or 18
limitation targets in the light of different national circumstances. 19
The Paris Agreement’s mitigation goal implies “to achieve a balance between anthropogenic emissions 20
by sources and removals by sinks of greenhouse gases in the second half of this century” (PA Article 4 21
para 1). The Paris Agreement provides for 5-yearly stocktakes in which Parties have to take collective 22
stock on progress towards achieving its purposes and its long-term goal in the light of equity and 23
available best science (PA Article 14). The first global stocktake is scheduled for 2023. (PA Article 14 24
para 3). 25
The Paris Agreement aims to make ‘finance flows consistent with a pathway towards low greenhouse 26
gas emissions and climate-resilient development’ (PA Article 2.1C). In keeping with the acknowledged 27
context of global sustainable development and poverty eradication, and the corresponding aims of 28
aligning finance and agreed differentiating principles as indicated above, “…the developed country 29
parties are to assist developing country parties with financial resources” (PA Article 9). The Green 30
Climate Fund (GCF), an operating entity of the UNFCCC Financial Mechanism to finance mitigation 31
and adaptation efforts in developing countries (GCF 2020), was given an important role in serving the 32
Agreement and supporting PA goals.. The GCF gathered pledges worth USD 10.3 billion, from 33
developed and developing countries, regions, and one city (Paris) (Antimiani et al. 2017; Bowman and 34
Minas 2019). Financing has since increased but remains short of the goal to mobilise USD100 billion 35
by 2020 (Chapter 15). 36
Initiatives contributing to the Paris Agreement goals include the Non-State Actor Zone for Climate 37
Action (NAZCA or now renamed as Global Climate Action) portal, launched at COP20 (December 38
2014) in Lima, Peru, to support city-based actions for mitigating climate change (Mead 2015) and 39
Marrakech Partnership for Global Climate Action which is a UNFCCC-backed series of events intended 40
to facilitate collaboration between governments and the cities, regions, businesses and investors that 41
must act on climate change. 42
