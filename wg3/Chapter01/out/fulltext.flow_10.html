In AR6 the IPCC employs a common risk framing across all three working groups and provides 3
guidance for more consistent and transparent usage (AR6 WGI Cross-Chapter Box 3 in Chapter 1; AR6 4
WGII 1.4.1; IPCC risk guidance). AR6 defines risk as “the potential for adverse consequences for 5
human or ecological systems, recognising the diversity of values and objectives associated with such 6
systems” (Annex I), encompassing risks from both potential impacts of climate change and human 7
responses to it (Reisinger et al. 2020). The risk framing includes steps for identifying, evaluating, and 8
prioritising current and future risks; for understanding the interactions among different sources of risk; 9
for distributing effort and equitable sharing of risks; for monitoring and adjusting actions over time 10
while continuing to assess changing circumstances; and for communications among analysts, decision-11
makers, and the public. 12
Climate change risk assessments face challenges including a tendency to mis-characterise risks and pay 13
insufficient attention to the potential for surprises (Weitzman 2011; Aven and Renn 2015; Stoerk et al. 14
2018). Concepts of resilience and vulnerability provide overlapping, alternative entry points to 15
understanding and addressing the societal challenges caused and exacerbated by climate change (AR6, 16
WGII, Chapter 1.2.1). 17
AR6 WGII devotes a full chapter (17) to ‘Decision-Making Options for Managing Risk’, detailing the 18
analytic approaches and drawing upon the Cynefin classification of Known, Knowable, Complex and 19
Chaotic systems (17.3.1). With deep uncertainty, risk management often aims to identify specific 20
combinations of response actions and enabling institutions that increase the potential for favourable 21
outcomes despite irreducible uncertainties (AR6 WGII Chapter 17 Cross-Chapter Box DEEP; also 22
(Marchau et al. 2019; Doukas and Nikas 2020). 23
Literature trying to quantify the cost of climate damages has continued to develop. Different 24
methodologies systematically affect outcomes, with recent estimates based on empirical approaches – 25
econometric measurements based on actual impacts – ‘categorically higher than estimates from other 26
approaches’ (Cross-Working Group Box ECONOMIC in WGII Chapter 16, Section 16.6.2). This, along 27
with other developments strengthen foundations for calculating a ‘social cost of carbon’. This informs 28
a common metric for comparing different risks and estimating benefits compared to the costs of GHG 29
reductions and other risk-reducing options (Section 1.7.1); emissions mitigation itself also involves 30
multiple uncertainties, which alongside risks can also involve potential opportunities (Section 1.7.3). 31
Simultaneously, the literature increasingly emphasises the importance of multi-objective risk 32
assessment and management (e.g., representative key risks in WGII Chapter 16), which may or may 33
not correlate with any single estimate of economic value (AR6 WGII 1.4.1; IPCC risk guidance). Given 34
the deep uncertainties and risks, the goals established (notably in the Paris Agreement and SDGs) reflect 35
negotiated outcomes informed by the scientific assessment of risks. 36
