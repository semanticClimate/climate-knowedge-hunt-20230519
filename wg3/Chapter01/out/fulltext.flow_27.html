Governments cannot singlehandedly fund the transition (Section 15.6.7 in Chapter 15), least of all in low-19
income developing countries with large sovereign debt and poor access to global financial markets. Long-20
term sources of private capital are required to close the financing gap across sectors and geographies 21
(Section 15.6.7 in Chapter 15). Future investment needs are greatest in emerging and developing economies 22
(Section 15.5.2 In Chapter 15) which already face higher cost of capital, hindering capacity to finance a 23
transition (Buhr et al. 2018; Ameli et al. 2020). Requisite North-South financial flows are impeded by both 24

There is often a disconnect between stated national climate ambition and finance flows, and overseas direct 35
investment (ODI) from donor countries may be at odds with national climate pledges such as NDCs. One 36
report found funds supported by foreign State-Owned Enterprises into 56 recipient countries in Asia and 37
Africa in 2014-2017 went mostly to fossil fuel-based projects not strongly aligned with low-carbon 38
priorities of recipient countries’ NDCs (Zhou et al. 2018). Similarly, Steffen and Schmidt (2019) found that 39
even within Multilateral Development Banks, ‘public- and private-sector branches differ considerably’, 40
with public-sector lending used mainly in non-renewable and hydropower projects. Political leadership is 41
therefore essential to steer financial flows to support low carbon transition (15.6). Voituriez er al. (2019) 42
identify significant mitigation potential if financing countries simply applied their own environmental 43
standards to their overseas investments. 44
