The literature shows increasing emphasis on policy packages, including those spanning the different levels 14
of niche/behaviour; existing regimes governing markets and public actors; and strategic and landscape 15
levels (Section 1.7.3). Chapters 13, 16, and 17 appraise policies for transformation in the context of 16
sustainable development, indicating the importance of policy as a driver at multiple levels and across many 17
actors, with potential for benefits as well as costs at many levels. 18
National-level legislation may be particularly important to the credibility and long-term stability of policy 19
to reduce the risks, and hence cost, of finance (Chapters 13, 15) and for encouraging private sector 20
innovation at scale (Chapter 16), for example if it offers greater stability and mid-term predictability for 21
carbon prices; Nash and Steurer (2019) find that seven national Climate Change Acts in European countries 22
all act as ‘living policy processes, though to varying extents’. 23
The importance of policy at multiple levels does not lessen the importance of international policy, for 24
reasons including long-term stability, equity, and scope, but examples of effective implementation policy 25
at international levels remain fewer and governance weaker (Chapter 14). 26
1.4.9
Legal framework and institutions
27
Institutions are rules and norms held in common by social actors that guide, constrain and shape human 28
interaction (IPCC 2018b). Institutions can be formal, such as laws and policies, or informal, such as norms 29
and conventions. Institutions can both facilitate or constrain climate policy-making and implementation in 30
multiple ways. Institutions set the economic incentives for action or inaction on climate change at national, 31
regional and individual levels (Dorsch and Flachsland 2017; Sullivan 2017). 32
