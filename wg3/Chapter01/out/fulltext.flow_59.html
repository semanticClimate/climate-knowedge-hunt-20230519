Just Transitions. Finally, whilst ‘transition’ frameworks may explain potential dynamics that could 12
transform systems, a multi-dimensional/multi-framework assessment underlines the motivation for ‘just 13
transitions’ (see subsection 1.6.2.3 in this chapter and section 4.5 in Chapter 4). This can be defined as 14
a transition from a high-carbon to a low-carbon economy which is considered sufficiently equitable for 15
the affected individuals, workers, communities, sectors, regions and countries (Jasanoff 2018; Newell 16
and Mulvaney 2013). As noted, sufficient equity is not only an ethical issue but an enabler of deeper 17
ambition for accelerated mitigation (Hoegh-Guldberg et al. 2019; Klinsky and Winkler 2018; 18
Urpelainen and Van de Graaf 2018). Perception of fairness influences the effectiveness of cooperative 19
action (Winkler et al. 2018), and this can apply to affected individuals, workers, communities, sectors, 20
regions and countries (Newell and Mulvaney 2013; Jasanoff 2018). 21
A ‘just transitions’ framing can also enable coalitions which integrate low carbon transformations with 22
concerns for climate adaptation (Patterson et al. 2018). All this explains the emergence of ‘just transition 23
Commissions’ in several of the more ambitious developed countries and complex social packages for 24
coal phase-out in Europe (Section 4.5 in Chapter 4; Sovacool et al. 2019; Green and Gambhir 2020), as 25
well as reference to the concept in the PA and its emphasis in the Talanoa dialogue and Silesia 26
declaration (1.2.2). 27
Whilst the broad concepts of ‘just transition’ have roots going back decades, its specific realisation in 28
relation to climate change is of course complex: Section 5 in Chapter 4 identifies at least eight distinct 29

1.9
Governing climate change
32
Previous sections have highlighted the multiple factors that drive and constrain climate action, the 33
complex interconnection between climate mitigation and other societal objectives, and the diversity of 34
analytical frames for interpreting these connections. Despite the complexities, there are signs of 35
progress including increased societal awareness, change in social attitudes, policy commitments by a 36
broad range of actors and sustained emission reductions in some jurisdictions. Nevertheless, emission 37
trends at the global level remains incompatible with the goals agreed in the Paris Agreement. 38
Fundamentally, the challenge of how best to urgently scale up and speed-up the climate mitigation effort 39
at all scales –from local to global – to the pace needed to address the climate challenge is that of 40
governance understood as ‘modes and mechanisms to steer society’ (Jordan et al. 2015). The concept 41
of governance encompasses the ability to plan and create the organisations needed to achieve a desired 42
goal (Güney 2017) and the process of interaction among actors involved in a common problem for 43
making and implementing decisions (Hufty 2012; Kooiman 2003). 44
