Integration. Politics is ultimately the way in which societies make decisions – which in turn, reflect 14
diverse forces and assumed frameworks. Effective policy requires understandings which combine 15
economic efficiency, ethics and equity, the dynamics and processes of large-scale transitions, and the 16
role of psychology and politics. No one framework is adequate to such a broad-ranging goal, nor are 17
single tools. Chapter 13 (Figure 13.6) presents a ‘framing’ table for policy instruments depending on 18
the extent to which they focus on mitigation per se or wider socio-economic development, and whether 19
they aim to shift marginal incentives or drive larger transitions. Holistic analysis needs to bridge 20
modelling, qualitative transition theories illuminated by case studies, and practice-based action research 21
(Geels et al. 2016). 22
These analytic frameworks also point to arenas of potential synergies and trade-offs (when broadly 23
known), and opportunities and risks (when uncertainties are greater), associated with mitigation. This 24
offers theoretical foundations for mitigation strategies which can also generate co-benefits. Climate 25
policy may help to motivate policies with beneficial synergies (such as the consumer cost savings from 26
energy efficiency, better forest management, transitions to cleaner vehicles) and opportunities (such as 27
stimulating innovation), by focusing on options for which the positives outweigh the negatives, or can 28
be made to through smart policy (e.g. Karlsson et al. 2020). More broadly, climate concerns may help 29
