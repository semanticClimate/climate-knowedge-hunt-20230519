1.8.1
Building on the SR1.5 assessment framework: feasibility and enabling conditions
7
While previous ARs dealt with the definition of alternative mitigation pathways mostly exploring the 8
technological potentials, latest research focused on what kind of mitigation pathways are feasible in a 9
broader sense, underlining the multi-dimensional nature of the mitigation challenge. Building on 10
frameworks introduced by Majone (1975) and Gilabert and Lawford-Smith (2012), SR1.5 introduced 11
multi-dimensional approaches to analysing ‘feasibility’ and ‘enabling conditions’, which AR6 develops 12
and applies broadly in relation to six ‘dimensions of feasibility assessment’ (
Figure 1.7
). Two reflect 13
the physical environment: 14
•
Geophysical, not only the global risks from climate change but also, for technology assessment, the 15
global availability of critical resources; 16
•
Environmental & ecological, including local environmental constraints and co-benefits of different 17
technologies and pathways 18
The other four dimensions correspond broadly to the four Analytic Frameworks outlined in Section 1.7: 19
•
Economic, particularly aggregate economic and financial indicators, and SDGs reflecting different 20
stages and goals of economic development; 21
•
Socio-cultural, including particularly ethical and justice dimensions, and social and cultural norms; 22
•
Technological, including innovation needs and transitional dynamics associated with new and 23
emergent technologies and associated systems; and 24
•
Institutional & political, including political acceptability, legal and administrative feasibility, and 25
the capacity and governance requirements at different levels to deliver sustained mitigation in the 26
wider context of sustainable development. 27
