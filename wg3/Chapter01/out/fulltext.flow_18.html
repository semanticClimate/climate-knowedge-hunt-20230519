Lessons. Important lessons can be drawn from the pandemic to climate change including the value of 7
forward-looking risk management, the role of scientific assessment, preparatory action and international 8
process and institutions (1.3, Chapter 5). There had been long-standing warnings of pandemic risks, and 9
precursors – with both pandemic and climate risks being identified by social scientists as ‘uncomfortable 10
knowledge’, or ‘unknown knowns’ which tend to be marginalised in practical policy (Rayner 2012; 11
Sarewitz 2020). This echoes long-standing climate literature on potential ‘high impact’ events, including 12
those perceived as low probability (Dietz 2011; Weitzman 2011). The costs of preparatory action, mainly 13
in those countries that had suffered from earlier pandemics were negligible in comparison, suggesting the 14
importance not just of knowledge but its effective communication and embodiment in society (Chapter 5). 15
(Klenert et al. 2020) offer five early lessons for climate policy, concerning: the cost of delay; the bias in 16
human judgement; the inequality of impacts; the need for multiple forms of international cooperation; and 17
finally, ‘transparency in value judgements at the science-policy interface’. 18
