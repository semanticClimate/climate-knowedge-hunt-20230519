The IMPs are organized around two dimensions: the level of ambition consistent with meeting Paris goals 4
and the scenario features (Figure 1.4). The IMPs explore different pathways potentially consistent with 5
meeting the long-term temperature goals of the Paris Agreement. As detailed in Section 3.2.5 of Chapter 3 6
and Chapter 4, a pathway of Gradual Strengthening of current policies (IMP-GS) to 2030, if followed by 7
very fast reductions, may stay below 2C. The IMP-NEG pathway, with somewhat deeper emission 8
cutbacks to 2030, might enable 1.5C to be reached but only after significant overshoot, through the 9
subsequent extensive use of CDR in the energy and the industry sectors to achieve net negative global 10
emissions, as discussed in Chapters 3, 12, 7, 6, and 10. 11
Three other IMPs illustrate different features of technology scenarios with more short term rapid emission 12
reductions, which could deliver outcomes compatible with the temperature range in the Paris agreement 13
without large overshoot. Based on the assessment in Section 5.3.3 of Chapter 5, one key mitigation strategy 14
would be to rely on the opportunities for reducing demand (IMP-LD). Chapter 6 and the Chapter 7-11 show 15
how energy systems based on accelerated deep renewable energy penetration and electrification can also 16
provide a pathway to deep mitigation (IMP-REN). Chapter 4, 17 and 3 provide insights how shifting 17
development pathways can lead to deep emission reductions and achieve sustainable development goals 18
(IMP-SP). 19
