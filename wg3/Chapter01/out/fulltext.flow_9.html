The atmosphere is a shared global resource and an integral part of the “global commons”. In the 9
depletion/restoration of this resource, myriad actors at various scales are involved, for instance, 10
individuals, communities, firms and states. Inter alia, international cooperation to tackle ozone 11
depletion and acid rain offer useful examples. AR5 noted that greater cooperation would ensue if 12
policies are perceived as fair and equitable by all countries along the spectrum of economic 13
development–implying a need for equitable sharing of the effort. A key takeaway from AR5 is that 14
climate policy involves value judgement and ethics. (IPCC 2014a Box TS.1 “People and countries have 15
rights and owe duties towards each other. These are matters of justice, equity, or fairness. They fall 16
within the subject matter of moral and political philosophy, jurisprudence, and economics.” p. 37). 17
International cooperation and collective action on climate change alongside local, national, regional and 18
global policies will be crucial to solve the problem, and this report notes cooperative approaches beyond 19
simple ‘global commons’ framings (Chapters 13, 14). 20
AR5 (all Working Group Reports) also underlined that climate policy inherently involves risk and 21
uncertainty (in nature, economy, society and individuals). To help evaluate responses, there exists a rich 22
suite of analytical tools, for example, cost-benefit analysis, cost-effectiveness analysis, multi-criteria 23
analysis, expected utility theory and catastrophe and risk models. All have pros and cons, and have been 24
further developed in subsequent literature and AR6 (next section). 25
