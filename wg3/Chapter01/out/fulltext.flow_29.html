1.4.6
Equity and fairness
1
Equity and fairness can serve as both driver and barrier to climate mitigation at different scales of 2
governance. Literature regularly highlights equity and justice issues as critical components in local politics 3
and international diplomacy regarding all SDG, such as goals for no poverty, zero hunger, gender equality, 4
affordable clean energy, reducing inequality, but also for climate action (Goal 13) (Marmot and Bell 2018; 5
Spijkers 2018). Equity issues help explain why it has proved hard to reach more substantive global 6
agreements, as it is hard to agree on a level of greenhouse gas mitigation (or emissions) and how to distribute 7
mitigation efforts among countries (Kverndokk 2018) for several reasons. First, an optimal trade-off 8
between mitigation costs and damage costs of climate change depends on ethical considerations, and 9
simulations from integrated assessment models using different ethical parameters producing different 10
optimal mitigation paths, see (IPCC 2018b and Section 3.6.1.2 in Chapter 3). Second, treaties that are 11
considered unfair may be hard to implement (Liu et al. 2017; Klinsky et al. 2017). Lessons from 12
experimental economics show that people may not accept a distribution that is considered unfair, even if 13
there is a cost of not accepting (Gampfer 2014). As equity issues are important for reaching deep 14
decarbonisation, the transition towards a sustainable development (Okereke 2018; Evans and Phelan 2016; 15
Heffron and McCauley 2018) depends on taking equity seriously in climate policies and international 16
negotiations (Martinez et al. 2019; Okereke and Coventry 2016; Klinsky et al. 2017). 17
Climate change and climate policies affect countries and people differently Low-income countries tend to 18
be more dependent on primary industries (agriculture, fisheries, etc.) than richer countries, and their 19
infrastructure may be less robust to tackle more severe weather conditions. Within a country, the burdens 20
may not be equally distributed either, due to policy measures implemented and from differences in 21
vulnerability and adaptive capacity following from e.g. income and wealth distribution, race and gender. 22
For instance, unequal social structures can result in women being more vulnerable to the effects of climate 23
change compared to men, especially in poor countries (Jost et al. 2016; Rao et al. 2019; Arora-Jonsson 24
2011). Costs of mitigation also differ across countries. Studies show there are large disparities of economic 25
impacts of NDCs across regions, and also between relatively similar countries when it comes to the level 26
of development, due to large differences in marginal abatement cost for the emission reduction goal of 27
NDCs (Akimoto et al. 2018; Fujimori et al. 2016; Edmonds et al. 2019; Hof et al. 2017). Equalizing the 28
burdens from climate policies may give more support for mitigation policies (Maestre-Andrés et al. 2019). 29
Taking equity into account in designing an international climate agreement is complicated as there is no 30
single universally accepted equity criterion, and countries may strategically choose a criterion that favours 31
them (Lange et al. 2007, 2010). Still, several studies analyse the consequences of different social 32
preferences in designing climate agreements, such as for instance inequality aversion, sovereignty and 33
altruism (Anthoff et al. 2010; Kverndokk et al. 2014). 34
International transfers from rich to poor countries to support mitigation and adaptation activities may help 35
equalizing burdens, as agreed upon in the UNFCCC (1992; Chapters 14 and 15) such that they may be 36
motivated by strategic as well as equity reasons (Kverndokk 2018; see also 1.4.4). 37
