A common criticism of international institutions is their limited (if any) powers to enforce compliance 5
(Zahar 2017). As a global legal institution, the PA has little enforcement mechanism (Sindico 2015), but 6
enforcement is not a necessary condition for an instrument to be legally binding (Bodansky 2016; Rajamani 7
2016). In reality implementation of specific commitments tends to be high once countries have ratified and 8
a Treaty or an Agreement is in force (Bodansky 2016; Rajamani 2016). Often, the problem is not so much 9
of 'power to enforce compliance or sanction non-compliance', but the level of ambition (Chapter 14). 10
However, whilst in most respects a driver, international cooperation has also been characterised as 11
‘organised hypocrisy’ where proclamations are not matched with corresponding action (Egnell 2010). 12
Various reasons for inadequate progress after 30 years of climate negotiations, have been identified 13
(Stoddard et al. 2021). International cooperation can also seem to be a barrier to ambitious action when 14
negotiation is trapped in ‘relative-gains’ calculus, seek to game the regime or gain leverage over one 15
another (Purdon 2017), or where states lower ambition to the ‘least common dominator’ to accommodate 16
participation of the least ambitious states (Falkner 2016). Geden (2016) and Dubash (2020) offer more 17
nuanced assessments. 18
International collaboration works best if an agreement can be made self-reinforcing with incentives for 19
mutual gains and joint action (Keohane and Victor 2016; Barrett 2016), but the structure of the climate 20
challenge makes this hard to achieve. The evidence from the Montreal Protocol on ozone depleting 21
substances and from the Kyoto Protocol on GHGs, is that legally binding targets have been effective in that 22
participating Parties complied with them (Albrecht and Parker 2019; Shishlov et al. 2016), and (for Kyoto) 23
these account for most of the countries that have sustained emission reductions for at least the past 10-15 24
years (Section 1.3.2; Section 2.2 in Chapter 2). However, such binding commitments may deter 25
participation if there are no clear incentives to sustain participation and especially if other growing emitters 26
are omitted by design, as with the Kyoto Protocol. Consequently the US refused to ratify (and Canada 27
withdrew), particularly on the grounds that developing countries had no targets; with participation in 28
Kyoto’s second period commitments declining further, the net result was limited global progress in 29
emissions under Kyoto (Scavenius and Rayner 2018; Bodansky 2016; Okereke and Coventry 2016) despite 30
full legal compliance in both commitment periods (chapter 14). 31
The negotiation of the Paris Agreement was thus done in the context of serious questions about how best 32
to structure international climate cooperation to achieve better results. This new agreement is designed to 33
side-step the fractious bargaining which characterised international climate cooperation (Marcu 2017). It 34
contains a mix of hard, soft and non-obligations, the boundaries between which are blurred, but each of 35
which plays a distinct and valuable role (Rajamani 2016). The provisions of the PA could encourage flexible 36
responses to changing conditions, but limit assurances of ambitious national commitments and their 37
fulfilment (Pickering et al. 2018). The extent to which this new arrangement will drive ambitious climate 38
policy in the long run remains to be seen (Chapter 14). 39
