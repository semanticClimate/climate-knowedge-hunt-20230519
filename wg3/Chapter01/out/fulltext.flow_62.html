Knowledge gaps
12
Despite huge expansion in the literature (Callaghan et al. 2020), knowledge gaps remain. Modeling still 13
struggles to bring together detailed physical and economic climate impacts and mitigation, with limited 14
representation of financial and distributional dynamics. There are few interdisciplinary tools which 15
apply theories of transition and transformation to questions of economic and social impacts, 16
compounded by remaining uncertainties concerning the role of new technological sets, international 17
instruments, policy and political evaluation. 18
One scan of future research needs suggests three priority areas (Roberts et al. 2020); 1. Human welfare 19
focused development (e.g. reducing inequality), 2. How the historic position of states within 20
international power relations conditions their ability to respond to climate change, 3. Transition 21
dynamics and the flexibility of institutions to drive towards low carbon development pathways. There 22
remain gaps in understanding how international dynamics and agreements filter down to affect 23
constituencies and local implementation. Literature on the potential for supply side agreements, in 24
which producers agree to restrict the supply of fossil fuels (e.g. Asheim et al. 2019) is limited but gaining 25
increasing academic attention. 26
Nature is under pressure both at land and at sea as demonstrated by declining biodiversity (IPBES 27
2019). Climate policies could increase the pressure on land and oceans (see IPCC 2019c,b), with 28
insufficient attention to relationships between biodiversity and climate agreements and associated 29
policies. IPBES aims to coordinate with the IPCC more directly, but literature will be required to 30
support these reports. 31
Compounding these gaps is the fact that socially oriented, agriculture-related options, where human and 32
non-human systems intersect most obviously, remain under-researched (e.g. Balasubramanya and Stifel 33
2020). Efforts to engage with policies here, especially framed around ecosystem services, have often 34
neglected their “practical fitness” in favor of focusing on their “institutional fitness”, which needs to be 35
addressed in future research (Stevenson et al. 2021). 36
The relative roles of short-term mitigation policies and long-term investments, including government 37
and financial decision-making tools, remains inadequately explored. Strategic investments may include 38
city planning, public transport, EV charging networks, and CCU/CCS. Understanding how international 39
treaties can increase incentives to make such investments is all the more salient in the aftermath of 40
COVID-19, on which research is necessarily young but rapidly growing. Finally, the economic, 41
institutional and political strategies to close the gap between NDCs, actual implementation, and 42
mitigation goals– informed by the PA and the UNFCCC Global Stocktake – require much further 43
research. 44
