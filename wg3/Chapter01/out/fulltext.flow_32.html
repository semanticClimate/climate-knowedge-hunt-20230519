Since 2016, the number of climate litigation cases has increased rapidly. UN Environment’s “Global 13
Climate Litigation Report: 2020 Status Review” (UNEP 2020) noted that between March 2017 and 1 July 14
2020, the number of cases nearly doubled with at least 1,550 climate cases filed in 8 countries. Several 15
important cases such as Urgenda Foundation v. The State of the Netherlands (“Urgenda”) and Juliana et al 16
v. United States (“Juliana”) have had ripple effects, inspiring other similar cases (Lin and Kysar 2020). 17
Numerous international climate governance initiatives engage national and subnational governments, 18
NGOs and private corporations, constituting a “regime complex” (Keohane and Victor 2011; Raustiala and 19
Victor 2004). They may have longer-run and second-order effects if commitments are more precise and 20
binding (Kahler 2017). However, without targets, incentives, defined baseline or monitoring, reporting, and 21
verification, they are not likely to fill the “mitigation gap” (Michaelowa and Michaelowa 2017). 22

Tackling climate change is often mentioned as an important reason for strong international co-operation in 24
the 21
st
century (Bodansky et al. 2017; Cramton et al. 2017b; Keohane and Victor 2016; Falkner 2016). 25
Mitigation costs are borne by countries taking action, while the benefits of reduced climate change are not 26
limited to them, being in economic terms “global and non-excludable”. Hence anthropogenic climate 27
change is typically seen as a global commons problem (Wapner and Elver 2017; Falkner 2016). Moreover, 28
the belief that mitigation will raise energy cost and may adversely affect competitiveness creates incentives 29
for free riding, where states avoid taking their fair share of action (Barrett 2005; Keohane and Victor 2016). 30
International cooperation has the potential to address these challenges through collective action (Tulkens, 31
2019) and international institutions offer opportunity for actors to engage in meaningful communication, 32
and exchange of ideas about potential solutions (Cole 2015). International cooperation is also vital for the 33
creation and diffusion of norms and the framework for stabilising expectations among actors (Pettenger 34
2016). 35
Some key roles of the UNFCCC have been detailed by its former heads (Kinley et al. 2021). In addition to 36
specific agreements (most recently the PA) it has enhanced transparency through reporting and data, and 37
generated or reinforced several important norms for global climate action including the principles of equity, 38
common but differentiated responsibility and respective capabilities, and the precautionary principles for 39
