Drivers and Constraints of Climate Mitigation and System
5
Transitions/Transformation
6
This section provides brief assessment of key factors and dynamics that drive, shape and or limit climate 7
mitigation in (i) Economic factors: which include sectors and services; trade and leakage; finance and 8
investment; and technological innovation; (ii) Socio-political issues; which include political economy; 9
social innovation and equity and fairness; and (iii) Institutional factors, which comprise policy, legal 10
frameworks and international co-operation. 11
AR 5 introduced six “enabling conditions” for shifting development pathways which are presented in 12
Chapter 4 of this report and some of which overlap with the drivers reviewed here. However, the 13
terminology of drivers and constraints have been chosen here to reflect the fact that each of these factors 14
can serve as an enabling condition or a constraint to ambitious climate action depending on the context and 15
how they are deployed. Often one sees the factors exerting both push and pull forces at the same time in 16
the same and across different scales. For example, finance and investments can serve as a barrier or an 17
enabler to climate action (Battiston et al. 2021). Similarly, political economy factors can align in favour of 18
ambitious climate action or act in ways that inhibit strong co-operation and low carbon transition. The other 19
key insight from the assessment of the system drivers and constraints undertaken below is that none of the 20
factors or conditions by themselves is more or less important than the others. In addition to being deeply 21
intertwined all the factors matter in different measures with each exacting more or less force depending on 22
prevailing social, economic, cultural and political context. Often achieving accelerated mitigation would 23
require effort to bring several of the factors in alignment in and across multiple levels of political or 24
governance scales. 25
1.4.1
Services, sectors and urbanisation
26
Human activities drive emissions primarily through the demand for a wide range of services such as food, 27
shelter, heating/cooling, goods, travel, communication, and entertainment. This demand is fulfilled by 28
various activities often grouped into sectors such as agriculture, industry and commerce. The literature 29
uses a wide range of sectoral definitions to organize data and analysis (Chapter 2). Energy sectors are 30
typically organised into primary energy producers, energy transformation processes (such as power 31
generation, fuel refining), and major energy users such as buildings, industry, transport (Chapters 2, 5). 32
Other research (Chapter 8) organizes data around interacting urban and rural human activities. Land-based 33
activities can be organized into agriculture, forestry, and other land use (AFOLU), or land use, land use 34
change and forestry (LULUCF) (Chapter 7). Each set of sectoral definitions and analysis offers its own 35
insights. 36
Sectoral perspectives help to identify and understand the drivers of emissions, opportunities for emissions 37
mitigation, and interactions with resources, other goals and other sectors, including the co-evolution of 38
systems across scales (Moss et al. 2016; Kyle et al. 2016; Mori et al. 2017; IPBES 2019). Interactions 39
between sectors and agents pursuing multiple goals is a major theme pervading this assessment. 40
