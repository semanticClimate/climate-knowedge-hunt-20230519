1.7.2.1
Ethics and values 3
A large body of literature examines the critical role of values, ethics, attitudes, and behaviours as 4
foundational frames for understanding and assessing climate action, sustainable development and 5
societal transformation (IPCC WGIII IPCC 2014a chapter 3). Most of this work is offered as a counter 6
point or critique to mainstream literature’s focus on safe-guarding of economic growth of nations, 7
corporations and individuals (Castree 2017; Gunster 2017). These perspectives highlight the dominance 8
of economic utilitarianism in western philosophical thought as a key driver for unsustainable 9
consumption and global environmental change (Hoeing et al. 2015; Popescu 2016). 10
Entrenching alternative values that promote deep decarbonisation, environmental conservation and 11
protection across all levels of society is then viewed as foundational component of climate resilient and 12
sustainable development and for achieving human rights, and a safe climate world (Jolly et al. 2015; 13
Evensen 2015; Popescu 2016; Tàbara et al. 2019). The UN Human Rights Office of the High 14
Commissioner has highlighted the potentially crucial role of human rights in relation to climate change 15
(UNHCR 2018). While acknowledging the role of policy, technology, and finance, the ‘managerialist’ 16
approaches that emphasise ‘technical governance’ and fail to challenge the deeper values that underpin 17
societies may not secure the deep change required to avert dangerous climate change and other 18
environmental challenges (Hartzell-Nichols 2014; Steinberger et al. 2020). 19
Social justice perspectives emphasise the distribution of responsibilities, rights, and mutual obligations 20
between nations in navigating societal transformations (Patterson et al. 2018; Gawel and Kuhlicke 2017; 21
Leach et al. 2018). Current approaches to climate action may fail to match what is required by science 22
because they tend to circumvent constraints on human behaviour, especially constraints on economic 23
interest and activity. Related literature explores governance models that are centred on environmental 24
limits, planetary boundaries and the moral imperative to prioritise the poor in earth systems governance 25
(Carley and Konisky 2020; Kashwan et al. 2020), with emphasis on trust and solidarity as foundations 26
for global co-operation on climate change (Jolly et al. 2015). A key obstacle is that the economic 27
interests of states tend to be stronger than the drivers for urgent climate action (Bain 2017). 28
Short-term interests of stakeholders is acknowledged to impede the reflection and deliberation needed 29
for climate mitigation and adaptation planning (Hackmann 2016; Herrick 2018; Sussman et al. 2016; 30
Schlosberg et al. 2017). Situationally appropriate mitigation and adaptation policies at both national 31
and international level may require more ethical self-reflection (Herrick 2018), including self-32
transcendent values such as universalism and benevolence, and moderation which are positively related 33
to pro-environmental behaviours (Howell and Allen 2017; Jonsson and Nilsson 2014; Katz-Gerro et al. 34
2015; Braito et al. 2017). 35
Another strong theme in the literature concerns recognition of interdependence including the intimate 36
relationship between humans and the non-human world (Hannis 2016; Gupta and Racherla 2018; 37
Howell and Allen 2017), with such ecological interdependence offered as an organising principle for 38
enduring transformation to sustainability. A key policy implication of this is moving away from valuing 39
nature only in market and monetary terms to strongly incorporating existential and non-material value 40
