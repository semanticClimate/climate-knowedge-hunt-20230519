Global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions continued to rise to 2019: the aggregate reductions 2
implied by current Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) to 2030 would still make it 3
impossible to limit warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot, and would only be compatible 4
with likely limiting warming below 2°C if followed by much steeper decline, hence limiting 5
warming to either level implies accelerated mitigation actions at all scales (robust evidence, high 6
agreement). Since the IPCC’s Fifth Assessment Report (AR5), important changes that have emerged 7
include the specific objectives established in the Paris Agreement of 2015 (for temperature, adaptation 8
and finance), rising climate impacts, and higher levels of societal awareness and support for climate 9
action. The growth of global GHG emissions has slowed over the past decade, and delivering the 10
updated NDCs to 2030 would turn this into decline, but the implied global emissions by 2030 exceed 11
pathways consistent with 1.5°C by a large margin, and are near the upper end of the range of modelled 12
pathways which keep temperatures likely below 2°C. Continuing investments in carbon-intensive 13
activities at scale will heighten the multitude of risks associated with climate change and impede societal 14
and industrial transformation towards low carbon development. Meeting the long-term temperature 15
objective in the Paris Agreement therefore implies a rapid turn to an accelerating decline of GHG 16
emissions towards ‘net zero’, which is implausible without urgent and ambitious action at all scales. 17
The unprecedented COVID-19 pandemic has had far-reaching impacts on the global economic and 18
social system, and recovery will present both challenges and opportunities for climate mitigation. {1.2, 19
1.3, 1.5, 1.6, Chapters 3 and 4}. 20
While there are some trade-offs, effective and equitable climate policies are largely compatible 21
with the broader goal of sustainable development and efforts to eradicate poverty as enshrined in 22
the 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) (robust evidence, high agreement). Climate mitigation 23
is one of many goals that societies pursue in the context of sustainable development, as evidenced by 24
the wide range of the SDGs. Climate mitigation has synergies and/or trade-offs with many other SDGs. 25
There has been a strong relationship between development and GHG emissions, as historically both per 26
capita and absolute emissions have risen with industrialisation. However, recent evidence shows 27
countries can grow their economies while reducing emissions. Countries have different priorities in 28
achieving the SDGs and reducing emissions as informed by their respective national conditions and 29
capabilities. Given the differences in GHG emissions contributions, degree of vulnerabilities and 30
impacts, as well as capacities within and between nations, equity and justice are important 31
considerations for effective climate policy and for securing national and international support for deep 32
decarbonisation. Achieving sustainable global development and eradicating poverty as enshrined in the 33
17 SDGS would involve effective and equitable climate policies at all levels from local to global scale. 34
Failure to address questions of equity and justice over time can undermine social cohesion and stability. 35
International co-operation can enhance efforts to achieve ambitious global climate mitigation in the 36
context of sustainable development. {1.4, 1.6, Chapters 2, 3, 4, 5, 13 and 17}. 37
The transition to a low carbon economy depends on a wide range of closely intertwined drivers 38
and constraints, including policies and technologies where notable advances over the past decade 39
have opened up new and large-scale opportunities for deep decarbonisation, and for alternative 40
development pathways which could deliver multiple social and developmental goals (robust 41
evidence, medium agreement). Drivers for and constraints against low carbon societal transition 42
comprise economic and technological factors (the means by which services such as food, heating and 43
shelter are provided and for whom, the emissions intensity of traded products, finance, and investment), 44
socio-political issues (political economy, equity and fairness, social innovation and behaviour change), 45
and institutional factors (legal framework and institutions, and the quality of international cooperation). 46
