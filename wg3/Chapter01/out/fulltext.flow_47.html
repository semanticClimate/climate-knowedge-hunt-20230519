1.7.1.3
Disequilibrium, complex systems and evolutionary approaches 14
Other approaches to aggregate evaluation draw on various branches of intrinsically non-equilibrium 15
theories (e.g. Chang 2014). These including long-standing theories from the 1930s (e.g. Schumpeter 16
1934a; Keynes 1936)
to understand situations of structurally under-employed resources, potential 17
financial instabilities (Minsky 1986), and related economic approaches which emphasise time 18
dimensions (e.g. recent reviews in Legrand and Hagemann 2017; Stern 2018). More recently 19
developing have been formal economic theories of endogenous growth building on eg. Romer (1986), 20
and developments of Schumpeterian creative destruction (Aghion et al. 2021) and evolutionary 21
economic theories which abandon any notion of full or stable resource utilisation even as a reference 22
concept (Nelson and Winter 1982; Freeman and Perez 1988; Carlsson and Stankiewicz 1991; Perez 23
2001; Freeman and Louçã 2001). 24
The latter especially are technically grounded in complex system theories (e.g. Arthur 1989, 1999; 25
Beinhocker 2007; Hidalgo and Hausmann 2009). These take inherently dynamic views of economies 26
as continually evolving systems with continuously unfolding and path-dependent properties, and 27
emphasise uncertainty in contrast to any predictable or default optimality. Such approaches have been 28
variously applied in policy evaluation (Walton 2014; Moore et al. 2018), and specifically for global 29
decarbonisation (e.g. Barker and Crawford-Brown 2014) using global simulation models. Because these 30
have no natural reference ‘least lost’ trajectory, they illustrate varied and divergent pathways and tend 31
to emphasise the diversity of possibilities and relevant policies, particularly linked to innovation and 32
potentially ‘sensitive intervention points’ (Farmer et al. 2019; see also section 1.7.3). They also illustrate 33
that different representations of innovation and financial markets together can explain why estimated 34
impacts of mitigation on GDP can differ very widely (potentially even in sign), between different model 35
types (Chapter 15, Section 15.6.3 and Box 15.7). 36
