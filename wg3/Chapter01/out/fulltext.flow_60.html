Climate change governance has been projected as conscious transformation at unprecedented scale and 1
speed involving a contest of ideas and experimentation across scales of authority and jurisdiction 2
(Hildén et al. 2017; Laakso et al. 2017; Gordon 2018; van der Heijden 2018; Kivimaa et al. 2017). Yet, 3
there remains a sense that achieving the urgent transition to a low carbon, climate resilient and 4
sustainable world requires significant innovation in governance (Hoffmann 2011; Stevenson and 5
Dryzek 2013; Aykut 2016). 6
Starting from an initial focus on multilateral agreements, climate change governance has long evolved 7
into a complex polycentric structure that spans from the global to national and sub-national levels, with 8
“multiple parallel initiatives involving a range of actors at different levels of governance” (Okereke et 9
al. 2009) and relying on both formal and informal networks and policy channels channels (Bulkeley et 10
al. 2014; Jordan et al. 2015). At the international level, implementation of the Paris Agreement and the 11
UNFCCC more broadly is proceeding in parallel with other activities in an increasingly diverse 12
landscape of loosely coordinated institutions, constituting ’regime complex’ (Keohane and Victor 13
2011), and new cooperative efforts demonstrate an evolution in the shifting authority given to actors at 14
different level of governance (Chan et al. 2018). 15
Multi-level governance has been used to highlight the notion that the processes involved in making and 16
implementing decisions on climate change is no longer the exclusive preserve of government actors but 17
rather involve a range of non-nation state actors such as cities, businesses, and civil society 18
organisations (Bäckstrand et al. 2017; Jordan et al. 2018; IPCC 2014a Chapter 13, 13.3.1 and 13.5.2). 19
Increased multi-level participation of subnational actors, along with a diversity of other transnational 20
and non-state actors have helped to facilitate increased awareness, experimentation, innovation, 21
learning and achieving benefits at multiple scales. Multi-level participation in governance systems can 22
help to build coalitions to support climate change mitigation policies (Roberts et al. 2018) and 23
fragmentation has the potential to take cooperative and even synergistic forms (Biermann et al. 2009) 24
However, there is no guarantee that multilevel governance can successfully deal with complex human-25
ecological systems (Biermann et al. 2017; York et al. 2005; Di Gregorio et al. 2019). Multi-level 26
governance can contribute to an extremely polarised discussion and policy blockage rather than 27
enabling policy innovation (Fisher and Leifeld 2019). Fragmented governance landscape may lead to 28
coordination and legitimacy gaps undermining the regime (Nasiritousi and Bäckstrand 2019). 29
Nevertheless, the realities of the ‘drivers and constraints’ detailed in Section 4 the “glocal” nature of 30
climate change, the divided authority in world politics, diverse preferences of public and private entities 31
across the spectrum, and pervasive suspicions of free riding, implying the challenge as how to 32
incrementally deepen cooperation in a polycentric global system, rather than seeking a single, integrated 33
governance (Keohane and Victor 2016). 34
Crucially, climate governance takes place in the context of embedded power relations, operating at 35
global, national and local context. Effective rules and institutions to govern climate change are more 36
likely to emerge when where power structures and interests favour action. However widespread and 37
enduring co-operation can only be expected to when the benefits outweigh the cost of cooperation and 38
when the interest of key actors are sufficiently aligned (Barrett 1994; Victor 2011; Finus and Rübbelke 39
2008; Tulkens 2019; Mainali et al. 2018). Investigating the distribution and role of hard and soft power 40
resources, capacities and power relations within and across different jurisdictional levels is therefore 41
important to uncover hindrances to effective climate governance (Marquardt 2017). Institutions at 42
international and national levels as also critical as they have the ability to mediate power and interest of 43
actors and sustain cooperation based on equity and fair rules and outcomes. Governance, in fact, helps 44
