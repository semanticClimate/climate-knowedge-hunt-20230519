Psychological and behavioural dimensions 26
AR5 emphasised that decision processes often include both deliberate (‘calculate the costs and 27
beneﬁts’) and intuitive thinking, the latter utilising emotion- and rule-based responses that are 28
conditioned by personal past experience, social context, and cultural factors (e.g. Kahneman 2003), and 29
that laypersons tend to judge risks differently than experts - for example, ‘intuitive’ reactions are often 30
characterised by biases to status quo and aversion to perceived risks and ambiguity (Kahneman and 31
Tversky 1979). Many of these features of human reasoning create ‘psychological distance’ from climate 32
change (Spence et al. 2012; Marshall 2014). These can impede adequate personal responses, in addition 33
to the collective nature of the problem, where such problems can take the form of ‘uncomfortable 34
knowledge’, neglected and so becoming ‘Unknown knowns’ (Sarewitz 2020).These decision processes, 35
and the perceptions that shape them, have been studied through different lenses from psychology 36
(Weber 2016) to sociology (Guilbeault et al. 2018), and media studies (Boykoff 2011). Karlsson and 37
Gilek (2020) identify science denialism and ‘decision thresholds’ as key mechanisms of delay. 38
Experimental economics (Allcott 2011) also helps explain why cost-effective energy efficiency 39
measures or other mitigation technologies are not taken up as fast or as widely as the benefits might 40
suggest, including procrastination and inattention, as “we often resist actions with clear long-term 41
