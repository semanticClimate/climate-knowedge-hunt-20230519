1.7.2.2
Equity and representation: international public choice across time and space 8
Equity perspectives highlight three asymmetries relevant for climate change (Okereke 2017; Okereke 9
and Coventry 2016; 1.4.6). Asymmetry in contribution highlights different contributions to climate 10
change both in historical and current terms, and apply both within and between states as well as between 11
generations (Caney 2016; Heyward and Roser 2016). Asymmetry in impacts highlight the fact that the 12
damages will be borne disproportionately across countries, regions, communities, individuals and 13
gender; moreover, it is often those that have contributed the least that stand to bear the greatest impact 14
of climate change (Shi et al. 2016; IPCC 2014a). Asymmetry in capacity highlights differences of power 15
between groups and nations to participate in climate decision and governance, including capacity to 16
implement mitigation and adaptation measures. 17
If attention is not paid to equity, efforts designed to tackle climate change may end up exacerbating 18
inequities among communities and between countries (Heffron and McCauley 2018). The implication 19
is that to be sustainable in the long run, mitigation involves a central place for consideration of justice, 20
both within and between countries (Chapters 4, 14). Arguments that the injustices following from 21
climate change are symptomatic of a more fundamental structural injustice in social relations, are taken 22
to imply a need to address the deeper inequities within societies (Routledge et al. 2018). 23
Climate change and climate policies affect countries and people differently, with the poor likely to be 24
more affected (1.6.1). Ideas of ‘just transition’ (outlined in 1.8.2.) often have a national focus in the 25
literature, but also imply that mitigation should not increase the asymmetries between rich and poor 26
countries, implying a desire for transitions which seek reduce (or at least avoid adverse) distributional 27
affects. Thus, it comes into play in the timing of zero emissions (chapter 3 and 14). International climate 28
finance in which rich countries finance mitigation and adaptation in poor countries is also essential for 29
reducing the asymmetries between rich and poor countries (1.6.3 and chapter 15). 30
Equity across generations also matters, i.e., the distribution between the present and future generations. 31
One aspect is discounting (1.7.1). Another approach has been to study the burdens on each generation 32
following from the transition to low-carbon economies (IPCC 2014a chapter 3, Cross Working Group 33
Box 3 in Chapter 12). Suggestions include shifting more investments into ‘natural capital’, so that future 34
generations will inherit less physical capital but a better environment, or financing mitigation efforts 35
today using governmental debt redeemed by future generations (Broome 2012; Heijdra et al. 2006; Karp 36
and Rezai 2014; Hoel et al. 2019). 37
