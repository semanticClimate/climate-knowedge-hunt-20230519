What the IMPs do and don’t do. The IMPs are, as their name implies, a set of scenarios meant to 1
illustrate some important themes that run through the entire WGIII assessment. They illustrate that the 2
climate outcomes that the individuals and society will face in the century ahead depend on individual 3
and societal choices In addition, they illustrate that there are multiple ways to successful achievement 4
of Paris long-term temperature goals. 5
IMPs are not intended to be comprehensive. They are not intended to illustrate all possible themes in 6
this report. They do not, for example attempt to illustrate the range of alternative socioeconomic 7
pathways against which efforts to implement Paris goals may be set, or to reflect variations in potential 8
regional development pathways. They do not explore issues around income distribution or 9
environmental justice, but assume implicitly that where and how action occurs can be separated from 10
who pays, in ways to adequately address such issues. They are essentially pathways of technological 11
evolution and demand shifts reflecting broad global trends in social choice. The IMPs do not directly 12
assess issues of realization linked to the “drivers and constraints” summarized in our previous section, 13
and the quantifications use, for the most part, models that are grounded mainly in the Aggregate 14
Economics Frameworks (section 7.1). As such they reflect primarily the geophysical, economic and 15
technological Dimensions of Assessment, but can be assessed in relation to the full set of Feasibility 16
criteria (section 1.8.1). 17
Together the IMPs provide illustrations of potential future developments that can be shaped by human 18
choices, including: Where are current policies and pledges leading? What is needed to reach specific 19
temperature goals under varying assumptions? What are the consequences of different strategies to meet 20
climate targets (i.e. demand-side strategy, a renewable energy strategy or a strategy with a role for net 21
negative emissions)? What are the consequences of delay? What are the implications for other SDGs of 22
various climate mitigation pathways? 23

1.6.1
The Climate Change and Development Connection
30
Climate change mitigation is one of many goals that societies pursue in the context of sustainable 31
development, as evidenced by the wide range of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). Climate 32
change and sustainable development as well as development more broadly, are interwoven along 33
multiple and complex lines of relationship (Fankhauser 2016; Gomez-Echeverri 2018a; Okereke and 34
Massaquoi 2017; Okereke et al. 2009), as highlighted in several previous IPCC reports (IPCC 2007, 35
2019a, 2018b, 2011a, 2014a). With its significant negative impact on natural systems, food security and 36
infrastructure, loss of lives and territories, species extinction, conflict health, among several other risks, 37
climate change poses a serious threat to development and wellbeing in both rich and poor countries 38
(IPCC 2019b, 2018b, 2007, 2011a, 2014a). Without serious efforts at mitigation and adaptation, climate 39
change could push millions further into poverty and limit the opportunities for economic development 40
(Chapter 4 and 17). It follows that ambitious climate mitigation is necessary to secure a safe climate 41
within which development and wellbeing can be pursued and sustained. 42
At the same time, rapid and largescale economic development (which has in the past driven climate 43
change through land use change and dependence on fossil fuels), is widely seen as needed to improve 44
global wellbeing and lift millions especially in low- and middle-income countries out of poverty 45
