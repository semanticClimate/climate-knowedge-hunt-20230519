Policy impacts
22
Transformation to different systems will hinge on conscious policy to change the direction in which energy, 23
land-use, agriculture and other key sectors develop (Bataille et al. 2016; Chapters 13, 16). Policy plays a 24
central role in in land-related systems (Chapter 7), urban development (Chapter 8), improving energy 25
efficiency in buildings (Chapter 9) and transport / mobility (Chapter 10), and decarbonising industrial 26
systems (Chapter 11). 27
Policy has been and will be central not only because greenhouse gas emissions are almost universally under-28
priced in market economies (Stern and Stiglitz 2017; World Bank 2019), and because of inadequate 29
economic incentives to innovation (Jaffe et al. 2005) but also due to various delay mechanisms (Karlsson 30
and Gilek 2020) and multiple sources of path-dependence and lock-in to existing systems (Section 1.8.2), 31
including “Infrastructure developments and long-lived products that lock societies into GHG-intensive 32
emissions pathways may be difficult or very costly to change, reinforcing the importance of early action 33
for ambitious mitigation (robust evidence, high agreement).” (AR5 p.18). 34
Many hundreds of policies have been introduced explicitly to mitigate GHG emissions, improve energy 35
efficiency or land use, or to foster low carbon industries and innovation, with demonstrable impact. The 36
role of policy to date has been most evident in energy efficiency (Sections 5.4 and 5.6 in Chapter 5) and 37
electricity (Chapter 6). The IPCC Special Report on Renewable Energy already found that “Government 38
policies play a crucial role in accelerating the deployment of RE technologies”, (IPCC 2011a, p. 24). Policy 39
packages since then have driven rapid expansion in renewables capacity and cost reductions (eg. through 40
