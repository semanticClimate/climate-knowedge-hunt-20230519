1.7.4.2
Socio-political and institutional approaches 8
Political and institutional dynamics shape climate change responses in important ways, not least because 9
incumbent actors have frequently blocked climate policy (1.4.5). Institutional perspectives probe 10
networks of opposition (Brulle 2019) and emphasise that their ability to block - as well as the ability 11
of others to foster low carbon transitions - are structured by specific institutional forms across countries 12
(Lamb and Minx 2020). National institutions have widely been developed to promote traditionally 13
fossil-fuel based sectors like electricity and transport as key to economic development, contributing to 14
carbon lock-in (Seto et al. 2016) and inertia (Rosenschöld et al. 2014). 15
The influence of interest groups on policy-making varies across countries. Comparative political 16
economy approaches tend to find that countries where interests are closely coordinated by governments 17
(‘coordinated market economies’) have been able to generate transformative change more than those 18
with a more arms-length, even combative relationship between interest groups and governments 19
(‘liberal market economies’) (Lachapelle and Paterson 2013; Meckling 2018; Ćetković and Buzogány 20
2016; Zou et al. 2016). ‘Developmental states’ often have the capacity for strong intervention but any 21
low-carbon interventions may be overwhelmed by other pressures and very rapid economic growth 22
(Wood et al. 2020a). 23
Institutional features affecting climate policy include levels and types of democracy (Povitkina 2018), 24
electoral systems, or levels of institutional centralisation (federal vs unitary states, presidential vs 25
parliamentary systems) (Steurer and Clar 2018; Clulow 2019; Lachapelle and Paterson 2013). Countries 26
that have constructed an overarching architecture of climate governance institutions (e.g. cross-27
department and multilevel coordination, and semi-autonomous climate agencies), are more able to 28
develop strategic approaches to climate governance needed to foster transformative change (Dubash 29
2021). 30
Access of non-governmental organisations (NGOs) to policy processes enables new ideas to be adopted, 31
but too close an NGO-government relation may stifle innovation and transformative action (Dryzek et 32
al. 2003). NGO campaigns on fracking (Neville et al. 2019) or divestment (Mangat et al. 2018) have 33
raised attention to ideas such as ‘stranded assets’ in policy arenas (Piggot 2018; Newell et al. 2020; 34
Paterson 2021; Green 2018). Attempts to depoliticise climate change may narrow the space for 35
democratic participation and contestation, thus impacting policy responses (Swyngedouw 2010, 2011; 36
Kenis and Lievens 2014). Some institutional innovations have more directly targeted enhanced public 37
deliberation and participation, notably in citizens’ climate assemblies (Howarth et al. 2020) and in the 38
use of legal institutions to litigate against those opposing climate action (Peel and Osofksy 2020). This 39
literature shows that transformative pathways are possible within a variety of institutional settings, 40
although institutional innovation will be necessary everywhere to pursue zero carbon transitions; see 41
also Chapters 4 (Section 4) and 13. 42
