Four Analytic Frameworks for understanding mitigation response

Climate change is unprecedented in its scope (sectors, actors and countries), depth (major 22
transformations) and timescales (over generations). As such, it creates unique challenges for analysis. 23
It has been called “the greatest market failure in history” (Stern 2007); the Perfect Moral Storm 24
(Gardiner 2006) and a “super wicked problem” (Lazarus 2009; Levin et al. 2012) - one which appears 25
difficult to solve through the traditional tools and assumptions of social organisation and analysis. 26
To complement the extensive literature on risks and decision-making under uncertainty reviewed in 27
AR6-WGII (notably, Chapter 19), this section summarises insights and developments in key analytic 28
frameworks and tools particularly relevant to understanding specific mitigation strategies, policies and 29
other actions, including explaining the observed if limited progress to date. Organised partly as reflected 30
in the quotes above, these include aggregated (principally, economic) frameworks to evaluate system-31
level choices; ethical perspectives on values and equity including stages of development and 32
distributional concerns; and transition frameworks which focus on the processes and actors involved in 33
major technological and social transitions. These need to be complemented by a fourth set of approaches 34
which shine more light on psychological/behavioural and political factors. All these frameworks are 35
relevant, and together they point to the multiple perspectives and actions required if the positive drivers 36
of emission reduction summarised in section 4 are to outweigh the barriers and overcome the 37
constraints. 38
