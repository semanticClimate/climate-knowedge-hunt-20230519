The parallel 2015 UN Addis Ababa Conference on Finance for Development, and its resulting Action 21
Agenda, aims to ‘address the challenge of financing … to end poverty and hunger, and to achieve 22
sustainable development in its three dimensions through promoting inclusive economic growth, 23
protecting the environment, and promoting social inclusion.’ The Conference recognises the significant 24
potential of regional co-operation and provides a forum for discussing the solutions to common 25
challenges faced by developing countries (15.6.4). 26
Alongside this, private and blended climate finance is increasing but is still short of projected 27
requirements consistent with Paris Agreement goals (15.3.2.1). The financing gap is particularly acute 28
for adaptation projects, especially in vulnerable developing countries. From a macro-regulatory 29
perspective, there is growing recognition that substantial financial value may be at risk from changing 30
regulation and technology in a low-carbon transition, with potential implications for global financial 31
stability (15.6.3). To date, the most significant governance development is the Financial Stability 32
Board’s Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosure (TCFD) and its recommendations that 33
investors and companies consider climate change risks in their strategies and capital allocation, so 34
investors can make informed decisions (TCFD 2018), welcomed by over 500 financial institutions and 35
companies as signatories, albeit with patchy implementation (1.4. 4; 15.6.3). 36
Talanoa Dialogue and Just Transition. As mandated at Paris COP21 and launched at COP23, the 37
‘Talanoa Dialogue’ (UNFCCC 2018a) emphasised holistic approaches across multiple economic 38
sectors for climate change mitigation. At COP24 also, the Just Transition Silesia Declaration, focusing 39
on the need to consider social aspects in designing policies for climate change mitigation was signed 40
by 56 heads of state (UNFCCC 2018b). This underlined the importance of aiming for a ‘just transition’ 41
in terms of reducing emissions, at the same time preserving livelihoods and managing economic risks 42
for countries and communities that rely heavily on emissions-intensive technologies for domestic 43
growth (Markkanen and Anger-Kraavi 2019), and for maintaining ecosystem integrity through nature-44
based solutions. 45
