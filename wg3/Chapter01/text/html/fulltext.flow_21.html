Civil society pressures for stronger action. Civic engagement increased leading up to the Paris Agreement 9
(Bäckstrand and Lövbrand 2019) and after. Youth movements in several countries show young people’s 10
awareness about climate change, evidenced by the school strikes for the climate (Hagedorn et al. 2019; 11
Buettner 2020; Walker 2020; Thackeray et al. 2020). Senior figures across many religions (Francis 2015; 12
IFEES 2015) stressed the duty of humanity to protect future generations and the natural world, and warned 13
about the inequities of climate change. Growing awareness of local environmental problems such as air 14
pollution in Asia and Africa (Karlsson et al. 2020), and the threat to indigenous people rights and existence 15
has also fuelled climate activism (Etchart 2017). Grass-root movements (Cheon and Urpelainen 2018; 16
Fisher et al. 2019), build political pressure for accelerating climate change mitigation, as does increasing 17
climate litigation (Setzer and Vanhala 2019; Chapters 13 and 14). 18

Global trends contrary to multilateral cooperation. State-centred politics and geopolitical/geo-economic 28
tensions seem to have become more prominent across many countries and issues (WEF 2019). In some 29
cases, multilateral cooperation could be threatened by trends such as rising populism, nationalism, 30
authoritarianism and growing protectionism (Abrahamsen et al. 2019), making it more difficult to tackle 31
global challenges including protecting the environment (Schreurs 2016; Parker et al. 2017; WEF 2019). 32
Transnational alliances. Partly countering this trend, cities, businesses, a wide range of other non-state 33
actors also have emerged with important international networks to foster mitigation. City-based examples 34
include the Cities Alliance in addressing climate change, Carbon Neutral Cities Alliance, the Covenant of 35
Mayors (chapter 8); there are numerous other alliances and networks such as those in finance (chapter 15), 36
technology (chapter 16), amongst many others (chapters 13, 14). 37

Determined Contributions (NDCs). Including updates until October 2021, these would imply global GHG 39
