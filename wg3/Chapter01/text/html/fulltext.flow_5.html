Accelerating mitigation to prevent dangerous anthropogenic interference within the climate 8
system will require the integration of broadened assessment frameworks and tools that combine 9
multiple perspectives, applied in a context of multi-level governance (robust evidence, medium 10
agreement). Analysing a challenge on the scale of fully decarbonising our economies entails integration 11
of multiple analytic frameworks. Approaches to risk assessment and resilience, established across IPCC 12
Working Groups, are complemented by frameworks for probing the challenges in implementing 13
mitigation. Aggregate Frameworks include cost-effectiveness analysis towards given objectives, and 14
cost-benefit analysis, both of which have been developing to take fuller account of advances in 15
understanding risks and innovation, the dynamics of emitting systems and of climate impacts, and 16
welfare economic theory including growing consensus on long-term discounting. Ethical frameworks 17
consider the fairness of processes and outcomes which can help ameliorate distributional impacts across 18
income groups, countries and generations. Transition and transformation frameworks explain and 19
evaluate the dynamics of transitions to low-carbon systems arising from interactions amongst levels, 20
with inevitable resistance from established socio-technical structures. Psychological, behavioural and 21
political frameworks outline the constraints (and opportunities) arising from human psychology and the 22
power of incumbent interests. A comprehensive understanding of climate mitigation must combine 23
these multiple frameworks. Together with established risk frameworks, collectively these help to 24
explain potential synergies and trade-offs in mitigation, imply a need for a wide portfolio of policies 25
attuned to different actors and levels of decision-making, and underpin ‘just transition’ strategies in 26
diverse contexts. {1.2.2, 1.7, 1.8}. 27
The speed, direction and depth of any transition will be determined by choices in the, 28
environmental, technological, economic, socio-cultural and institutional realms (robust evidence, 29
high agreement). Transitions in specific systems can be gradual or rapid and disruptive. The pace of a 30
transition can be impeded by ‘lock-in’ generated by existing physical capital, institutions, and social 31
norms. The interaction between power, politics and economy is central in explaining why broad 32
commitments do not always translate to urgent action. At the same time, attention to and support for 33
climate policies and low carbon societal transition has generally increased, as the impacts have become 34
more salient. Both public and private financing and financial structures strongly affect the scale and 35
balance of high and low carbon investments. COVID-19 has strained public finances, and integrating 36
climate finance into ongoing recovery strategies, nationally and internationally, can accelerate the 37
diffusion of low carbon technologies and also help poorer countries to minimise future stranded assets. 38
Societal & behavioural norms, regulations and institutions are essential conditions to accelerate low 39
carbon transitions in multiple sectors, whilst addressing distributional concerns endemic to any major 40
transition. {1.3.3, Cross-Chapter Box 1 in this chapter, 1.4, 1.8, Chapters 2-4 and 15}. 41
