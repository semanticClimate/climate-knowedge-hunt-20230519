Integrating analyses. The response to COVID-19 also reflects the relevance of combining multiple 7
analytic frameworks spanning economic efficiency, ethics and equity, transformation dynamics, and 8
psychological and political analyses (Section 1.7). As with climate impacts, not only has the global burden 9
of disease been distributed unevenly, but capabilities to prevent and treat disease were asymmetrical and 10
those in greatest vulnerability often had the least access to human, physical, and financial resources (Ruger 11
and Horton 2020). ‘Green’ versus ‘brown’ recovery has corresponding distributional consequences 12
between these and ‘green’ producers, suggesting need for differentiated policies with international 13
coordination (Le Billon et al. 2021). This illustrates the role of ‘just transition’ approaches to global 14
responses including the value of integrated, multi-level governance (Section 1.7 in this Chapter, Section 15
4.5 in Chapter 4 and Section 17.1 in Chapter 17). 16
Crises and opportunities: the wider context for mitigation and transformation. The impacts of 17
COVID-19 have been devastating in many ways, in many countries, and may distract political and financial 18
capacity away from efforts to mitigate climate change. Yet, studies of previous post-shock periods suggest 19
that waves of innovation that are ready to emerge can be accelerated by crises, which may prompt new 20
behaviours, weaken incumbent (‘meso-level’) systems, and prompt rapid reforms (Section 1.6.5; Roberts 21
and Geels 2019a). Lessons from the collective effort to 'flatten the curve’ during the pandemic, illustrating 22
aspects of science-society interactions for public health in many countries, may carry over to climate 23
mitigation, and open new opportunities (Section 5.1.2 in Chapter 5). COVID-19 appears to have accelerated 24
the emergence of renewable power, electromobility and digitalisation (Newman 2020; Sectiopn 5.1.2 in 25
Chapter 5, Section 6.3 in Chapter 6, Section 10.2 in Chapter 10). Institutional change is often very slow but 26
major economic dislocation can create significant opportunities for new ways of financing and enabling 27
‘leapfrogging’ investment to happen (Section 10.8 in Chapter 10). Given the unambiguous risks of climate 28
change, and consequent stranded asset risks from new fossil fuel investments (Box 6.11), the most robust 29
recoveries are likely to be those which emerge on lower carbon and resilient pathways (Obergassel et al. 30
2020). Noting the critical global post-COVID-19 challenge as the double-impact of heightened credit risk 31
in developing countries, along with indebtedness in developed countries, (Hourcade et al. 2021a) estimate 32
that a ‘multilateral’ sovereign guarantee structure to underwrite low carbon investments could leverage 33
projects up to 15 times its value, contributing to shifting development pathways consistent with the SDGs 34
and Paris goals. 35
COVID- 19 can thus be taken as a reminder of the urgency of addressing climate change, a warning of the 36
risk of future stranded assets (Rempel and Gupta 2021; and Chapter 17), but also an opportunity for a 37
cleaner recovery. 38
